What other infections can E. coli lead to?

E. coli can lead to many other infections such as urinary tract infections, respiratory illness, and pneumonia. It can also cause severe stomach pain, bloody diarrhea, and kidney failure. National institutions of health say, “Most E. coli strains are harmless, but some can cause diarrhea through contact with contaminated food or water while other strains can cause urinary tract infections, respiratory illness and pneumonia.” Another similar article by Medscape also talks about the infections that come with e coli but came as a pathogen. Medscape says, “Common infections with E coli as a pathogen include cholecystitis, bacteremia, cholangitis, urinary tract infection (UTI), traveler's diarrhea, pneumonia, and neonatal meningitis.”

How does E. Coli Damage your intestines?

When a person gets E. coli, they start seeing symptoms like constant diarrhea, vomiting, cramps in their stomach and etc. This is all caused by E. coli because it damages the intestines of the human body. E. coli creates a toxin which damages the lining of the intestines which cause all of these symptoms. Kids Health says, “coli bacteria make a toxin (a poisonous substance) that can damage the lining of the small intestine. This can lead to bad stomach cramps, vomiting, and diarrhea (often with blood in it). When that happens, people can get dehydrated.” This explains how E. coli can damage a human’s intestines, and how when these symptoms start to come in, people also lose lots of water in their body because they vomit and excrete blood from their body which ends up in them getting dehydrated.

Why Are Cattle the Main Carriers of E. Coli?

Cattle or cows are the main carriers of E. coli because they live in certain environments where they drink from the water troughs which are a conduit for the spread of the E. coli toxin in cattle, and this spreads to people as well. Science daily says, “A major study reveals for the first time that water troughs on farms are a conduit for the spread of toxic E. coli in cattle, which can then spread the pathogen to people through bacteria in feces” This explains how cattle get the toxin of E. coli spread in them. Another study by CDC.gov says, “E. coli O157 is naturally found in the intestinal tracts of many farm animals, including healthy cattle, sheep, and goats. Animals can carry E. coli O157 and shed the germs in their stool but still appear healthy and clean.” This explains that cattle can normally carry E. coli because it is found in their intestinal tracts even if they are healthy.

Why does E. Coli become resistant to all antibiotics so rapidly?

E. coli can alter its genetic makeup very quickly through the process of mutations in normal cellular genes, by doing this they are able to get foreign-resistance genes from the environment they are present in, or by a mix of those functions. Medical Xpress says, “Bacteria such as Escherichia coli can alter their genetic makeup with astonishing rapidity. They do this, said DebRoy, ‘through mutations in normal cellular genes, by acquiring foreign-resistance genes from the environment or by a combination of those mechanisms.’” This tells us how E.coli can alter its genetic makeup so quickly to get foreign-resistance genes to antibiotics.
